## 1. MARKET ANALYSIS

### 1.1 Why Education Needs Anonymization

Educational institutions handle sensitive personal information daily:
- Student names, addresses, dates of birth
- Parent/guardian contact information
- Academic records and grades
- Health information and special needs documentation
- Staff employment records
- Financial data (scholarships, fees, payments)

**Key Challenges:**
- GDPR and data protection laws apply to all EU schools
- FERPA (USA), COPPA (children's data), and country-specific regulations
- Sharing data with external parties (researchers, contractors)
- Using AI tools for teaching while protecting student privacy
- Publishing case studies without exposing identities

### 1.2 Target Audience

**Primary:**
- Schools (primary, secondary, vocational)
- Universities and colleges
- Online learning platforms
- Educational publishers
- Research institutions

**Secondary:**
- Education ministries and departments
- EdTech companies
- School IT administrators
- Teachers and professors using AI tools
- Educational researchers

### 1.3 Geographic Scope

All countries with focus on:
- European Union (strict GDPR requirements)
- United Kingdom
- North America (USA, Canada)
- Australia and New Zealand
- Asia-Pacific (Singapore, Japan, South Korea)

### 2.4 Anonymization Methods

Five options explained simply:

| Method | What It Does | When to Use |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| **Replace** | Swaps real data with fake but realistic data | Sharing documents externally while keeping readability |
| **Redact** | Removes data completely, shows [REDACTED] | Official reports where data must be hidden |
| **Hash** | Creates a unique code from the data (SHA-256) | Research where you need to track patterns without knowing names |
| **Encrypt** | Scrambles data so only you can unscramble it (AES-256) | When you need to restore original data later |
| **Mask** | Partially hides data (Jo** D**) | Quick previews where some context is helpful |

## 5. CONTENT GUIDELINES

### 5.1 Language and Tone

**DO:**
- Use simple words ("protect" not "safeguard")
- Short sentences (15 words or less when possible)
- Active voice ("The app protects your data")
- Explain technical terms when used

**DON'T:**
- Use jargon without explanation
- Make claims not supported by product documentation
- Invent features that don't exist
- Promise specific compliance outcomes

### 5.2 Visual Guidelines

**Images:**
- Simple diagrams showing workflow
- Screenshots of actual products
- Icons for features (shield for security, globe for languages)
- Diverse representation in any people images

**Colors:**
- Primary: Professional blue (trust, education)
- Secondary: Green (safety, approval)
- Accent: Orange (call-to-action)
- Background: White/light gray (clean, clear)

### 5.3 Accessibility

- Alt text for all images
- Sufficient color contrast
- Keyboard navigation
- Clear heading hierarchy
- Simple language (aim for Grade 8 reading level)
